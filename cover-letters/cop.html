How About Some Homegrown Software?

I’ve been developing and maintaining complex web applications for more than twenty years, since I taught myself to code here in Portland while attending Grant High School. Even before graduating, I completed several paid contracts developing database-driven web apps using PHP, MySQL, ActionScript (Flash), JavaScript, and, of course, HTML/CSS, often contributing UX and UI design to the projects.

I continued doing contract web development while studying Computer Engineering at Santa Clara University, interning at PayPal, and then joining a social games startup in San Francisco where I wrote mostly Python and JavaScript. That company was acquired by Google, where I built a friend recommender service for Google+, the failed social network, worked on an iOS photo sharing application, and wrote more Python and JavaScript while maintaining YouTube’s playlist management page.

In the years since, I’ve worked across the spectrum of company size, from two-person startups doing full-stack application development, to Series-A funded startups doing database scaling work. I’ve even built internal tools for Facebook’s design teams. No matter the size of the company, I’ve always enjoyed projects where I can contribute to UX and UI design — understanding the user’s needs and building a delightful interface to meet them is deeply satisfying, and I have an unusually broad skillset that includes Linux server management, shell scripting, Docker, cloud services like AWS and Google Cloud, SQL, NoSQL, and graph databases, several server-side scripting languages (Python, JavaScript, Ruby), as well as several client-side platforms (iOS, web, and Android).

And as the son of an English teacher and journalist, documenting my work has always come easily.

Working for the City of Portland would add a new dimension of satisfaction—that of being in service to the city that I was raised in, that I’m proud to call home again. I would be delighted to discuss solving problems for the City’s Water Bureau team!

Sincerely,
Cameron Boehmer
